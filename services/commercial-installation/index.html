What We Install

Every commercial electrical installation starts with a set of plans and ends with a facility that has power where it needs to be, built to code, and cleared by inspection. What happens between those two points is where most contractors either deliver or create problems.

Purple Current handles the full electrical scope on commercial, industrial, and multi-family projects across Tampa Bay. That includes:

Power distribution and branch circuits.

Main service entrances, distribution panels, three-phase systems, dedicated circuits for equipment, and motor connections. We size everything to the actual load requirements of the building, not to a generic spec. If capacity needs change during construction, we catch it in planning, not during the rough-in.

Panel Upgrades & Service Upgrades Power Distribution Upgrades

Lighting rough-in and installation.

Interior and exterior commercial lighting, including controls, dimming systems, and code-compliant fixture layouts. We coordinate fixture placement with the architect's reflected ceiling plan and handle everything from conduit to final trim.

Commercial Lighting Installation

Emergency and exit lighting.

Battery backup units, central inverter systems, and photometric layouts that satisfy occupancy requirements. This isn't a line item to check off at the end of a project. It gets planned, installed, and inspected as part of the full electrical scope.

Emergency Lighting Installation

Fire alarm conduit and low-voltage rough-in.

We install the conduit and wiring infrastructure for fire alarm systems, data networks, access control, and security cameras. The signal and termination work is typically handled by the specialty contractor. We make sure they have clean, code-compliant pathways to work with.

Equipment connections and dedicated circuits.

Commercial kitchens, manufacturing floors, server rooms, medical equipment. Whatever the facility needs connected, we provide the correct circuit type, wire gauge, and overcurrent protection for the load.

Pre-Construction and Planning

The electrical scope starts before anyone pulls wire. We review the construction drawings, run load calculations, identify coordination points with other trades, and flag anything that looks like it will cause a problem during the build. Permit applications go in early. Material procurement happens before the job starts, not the week of.

For projects in Tampa Bay, we work with the local AHJ, whether that's the City of Tampa Building Department, Pasco County Building Services, or any of the other jurisdictions across our nine-county service area. We know who reviews what, how long it takes, and what they look for.

2

Rough-In

Rough-in is where the electrical system takes shape inside the walls, ceilings, and floors before everything gets closed up. Conduit runs, wire pulls, box placement, panel mounting, grounding, and bonding all happen during this phase.

This is also the phase where coordination with the GC and other subcontractors matters most. If the plumber moves a line and nobody tells the electrician, you get conflicts that cost time and money at trim-out. We stay on the coordination schedule and communicate changes when they happen, not after the drywall is up.

3

Trim-Out and Final

Trim-out is the finish phase: devices, fixtures, covers, and connections go in. Panels get labeled. Circuits get tested. Then we schedule the final inspection.

Our goal at final inspection is a clean pass. That means the work is right, the documentation is right, and the inspector can see both without digging. We don't treat inspection day as a surprise.

Why Commercial Installation Requires a Commercial Contractor

The gap between residential and commercial electrical work is wider than most people think. Three-phase power, higher voltages, stricter inspection standards, AHJ coordination, fire alarm integration, and NEC requirements that change based on occupancy type are all part of the job on a commercial site. A contractor who does houses during the week and picks up a commercial job on the side is operating in an environment they don't live in.

The problems show up in predictable places. Inspection failures from code misses on three-phase systems. Schedule delays because the contractor didn't know how to work within a GC's coordination timeline. Change orders from scope that should have been caught during plan review. These aren't rare occurrences. They're what happens when the contractor isn't built for the work.

Purple Current does commercial electrical work exclusively. Our crews are trained for it, our processes are built around it, and our 15 years in Tampa Bay mean we know the local jurisdictions, the inspection standards, and the coordination demands that come with commercial construction.
